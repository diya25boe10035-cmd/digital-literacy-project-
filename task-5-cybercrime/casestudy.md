Case Study:  Ransomware 

Ransomware is a sophisticated form of cyber-extortion where malicious software hides a victim’s digital files, 
rendering them inaccessible until a ransom, typically in cryptocurrency- anonymous and untraceable, is paid.
Unlike traditional theft, the goal is not just to steal data, but to hold it hostage.

The crime typically unfolds in four distinct stages:


1.	Infiltration: Attackers gain entry through "Phishing" emails containing malicious attachments, exploiting unpatched software vulnerabilities, or using stolen Remote Desktop Protocol credentials.

2.	Deployment: Once inside, the malware moves across the network to identify high-value targets, such as servers and backup drives.

3.	Encryption: The software executes a cryptographic lock on the files. The victim suddenly finds their documents, databases, and systems replaced by encrypted versions with unfamiliar extensions.

4.	Extortion: A "ransom note" appears on the screen, providing instructions on how to pay for the decryption key, often raising the stakes by threatening to leak sensitive data if the deadline is missed.


While individuals are targeted, criminals mainly focus on "Big Game Hunting"—targeting institutions that cannot afford downtime, such as hospitals, universities, and government agencies so that the victims are more likely to comply due to the time constraint and higher casulty rates.

The consequences are devastating. Beyond the financial loss of the ransom which does not guarantee data recovery, organizations face massive operational paralysis, permanent data loss, and severe reputational damage. For students and researchers, this can mean the total loss of years of academic work and compromised personal records, making offline backups a critical necessity.
